I am a PhD candidate in **Sociology** at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, and a **Population Science** pre-doctoral trainee at the Carolina Population Center. I received a BA in Social Research and Public Policy from New York University Abu Dhabi, in the UAE. 

Broadly, I study **mobility and migration** within and across borders, space, organizations, and labor markets.

I’m interested in a number of topics:

- **how people move** through the lens of duration, destinations, frequency, and direction of moves
- the role of **temporariness** — in contracts, visas, and duration of stay — in how people move and imagine their futures, and how this shifts with time
- the role of **context** (workplace and places) in shaping patterns of staying and leaving
- the consequences of individuals’ moves for themselves, connected persons, and societies
- globalization and the role of **organizations** in the in- and out-sourcing labor

I ask these questions in the context of both **place and labor markets**, and the interlinkage between spatial mobility and labor market mobility.

I think of mobility and migration as inherently **dynamic**.

I have applied a range of methods, both quantitative and qualitative, with recent work being largely quantitative. I’m particularly interested in research design and in the **measurement of mobility and migration**, as well as data visualization. My current work-in-progress uses longitudinal survey data and administrative data.

My dissertation highlights the rhythms of migration in a national context and its consequences for the study of incorporation; centers the role of organizations in the mobility and migration of individuals between places; and underscores complementary perspectives and methods in the study of labor market mobility, residential mobility, and migration.
